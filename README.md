# AMF-Climate-Gradient-

This repository contains the code and resources related to my first dissertation chapter and the paper, "Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungal Communities in Winegrapes Along an Edapholimatic Gradient of the Western USA."

# Overview

Project Summary: Arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi form mutualistic relationships with over 80% of terrestrial plants, enhancing nutrient and water uptake in exchange for plant-derived carbohydrates and lipids. AM fungi colonize roots, forming specialized structures like arbuscules that facilitate nutrient exchange. Their abundance and diversity are influenced by soil properties, such as pH and texture, which affect nutrient mobility, colonization success, and hyphal development. Climatic factors, including precipitation and temperature, further shape AM fungal communities by altering soil organic matter, moisture availability, and nutrient cycling. Additionally, plant traits like root architecture and genetic variation influence AM fungal associations, with grapevine rootstocks offering an ideal system to explore these relationships due to their large-diameter fine roots, low root density and few or short root hairs. This study investigates AM fungal abundance and diversity in grapevines across 11 vineyards spanning a 1,300 km gradient along the U.S. West Coast. We aim to identify how soil physical and chemical properties influence AM fungal communities by sampling diverse soils (e.g., silty clay loam to gravelly loam) and climates (temperate oceanic to Mediterranean). Using root staining and high-throughput sequencing, we will evaluate fungal colonization and diversity in grapevines while assessing the role of plant genotype in shaping AM fungal communities. By uncovering these interactions, this research will provide insights into adaptive vineyard management strategies, particularly under changing climatic and edaphic conditions.

Materials and methods: This study was conducted across 11 vineyards spanning three geographical regions—Oregon, Northern California, and Central California—representing diverse rootstocks, grape varieties, and environmental conditions. Thirty-year climate normals (1991-2020) were obtained from PRISM datasets. In March 2022, soil samples (0-30 cm) were collected from three to eighteen plots per vineyard, yielding a total of 69 samples. During grapevine veraison in July 2022, root samples were collected from three vines per plot across the same range of plots, totaling 207 root samples. Similarly, leaf samples were simultaneously collected at veraison, resulting in 69 leaf samples. Leaf samples collected at veraison were analyzed for nutrient concentrations to determine grapevine nutrient status. Fine roots were cleaned and split for two analyses: roots stored in 70% ethanol were cleared and stained for AM fungal abundance (hyphae, arbuscules, vesicles), while roots stored at -80°C underwent DNA extraction and ITS-2 amplicon sequencing for AM fungal diversity analysis. At harvest, grape quality data—including berry weight, total soluble solids (°Brix), pH, and titratable acidity—were collected to assess vine performance and fruit quality. Fresh, sieved (4 mm) soil samples were used for gravimetric moisture content, water-holding capacity, and mineralizable ammonium (NH4+) and nitrate (NO3-) concentrations. Air-dried, sieved (2mm) soil samples were analyzed for pH, electrical conductivity (EC), available phosphate (PO4-3), permanganate oxidizable carbon (POXC), microbial respiration, and soil texture. Micronutrient (i.e., K, Na, Ca, Mg, CEC, Zn, Mn, Cu, Fe) soil concentration and cation exchange capacity (CEC) was determined via DPTA extraction and ICP-AES at UC-Davis Analytical Lab. Leaf macro- and micronutrient concentrations (i.e., N, P, K, S, B, Ca, Mg, Zn, Mn, Fe, and Cu) were determined at UC-Davis Analytical Lab.
